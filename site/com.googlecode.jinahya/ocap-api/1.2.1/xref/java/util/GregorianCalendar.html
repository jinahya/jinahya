42  /** 
43   * <code>GregorianCalendar</code> is a concrete subclass of
44   * {@link Calendar}
45   * and provides the standard calendar used by most of the world.
46   *
47   * <p>
48   * The standard (Gregorian) calendar has 2 eras, BC and AD.
49   *
50   * <p>
51   * This implementation handles a single discontinuity, which corresponds by
52   * default to the date the Gregorian calendar was instituted (October 15, 1582
53   * in some countries, later in others).  The cutover date may be changed by the
54   * caller by calling <code>setGregorianChange()</code>.
55   *
56   * <p>
57   * Historically, in those countries which adopted the Gregorian calendar first,
58   * October 4, 1582 was thus followed by October 15, 1582. This calendar models
59   * this correctly.  Before the Gregorian cutover, <code>GregorianCalendar</code>
60   * implements the Julian calendar.  The only difference between the Gregorian
61   * and the Julian calendar is the leap year rule. The Julian calendar specifies
62   * leap years every four years, whereas the Gregorian calendar omits century
63   * years which are not divisible by 400.
64   *
65   * <p>
66   * <code>GregorianCalendar</code> implements <em>proleptic</em> Gregorian and
67   * Julian calendars. That is, dates are computed by extrapolating the current
68   * rules indefinitely far backward and forward in time. As a result,
69   * <code>GregorianCalendar</code> may be used for all years to generate
70   * meaningful and consistent results. However, dates obtained using
71   * <code>GregorianCalendar</code> are historically accurate only from March 1, 4
72   * AD onward, when modern Julian calendar rules were adopted.  Before this date,
73   * leap year rules were applied irregularly, and before 45 BC the Julian
74   * calendar did not even exist.
75   *
76   * <p>
77   * Prior to the institution of the Gregorian calendar, New Year's Day was
78   * March 25. To avoid confusion, this calendar always uses January 1. A manual
79   * adjustment may be made if desired for dates that are prior to the Gregorian
80   * changeover and which fall between January 1 and March 24.
81   *
82   * <p>Values calculated for the <code>WEEK_OF_YEAR</code> field range from 1 to
83   * 53.  Week 1 for a year is the earliest seven day period starting on
84   * <code>getFirstDayOfWeek()</code> that contains at least
85   * <code>getMinimalDaysInFirstWeek()</code> days from that year.  It thus
86   * depends on the values of <code>getMinimalDaysInFirstWeek()</code>,
87   * <code>getFirstDayOfWeek()</code>, and the day of the week of January 1.
88   * Weeks between week 1 of one year and week 1 of the following year are
89   * numbered sequentially from 2 to 52 or 53 (as needed).
90   *
91   * <p>For example, January 1, 1998 was a Thursday.  If
92   * <code>getFirstDayOfWeek()</code> is <code>MONDAY</code> and
93   * <code>getMinimalDaysInFirstWeek()</code> is 4 (these are the values
94   * reflecting ISO 8601 and many national standards), then week 1 of 1998 starts
95   * on December 29, 1997, and ends on January 4, 1998.  If, however,
96   * <code>getFirstDayOfWeek()</code> is <code>SUNDAY</code>, then week 1 of 1998
97   * starts on January 4, 1998, and ends on January 10, 1998; the first three days
98   * of 1998 then are part of week 53 of 1997.
99   *
100  * <p>Values calculated for the <code>WEEK_OF_MONTH</code> field range from 0
101  * to 6.  Week 1 of a month (the days with <code>WEEK_OF_MONTH =
102  * 1</code>) is the earliest set of at least
103  * <code>getMinimalDaysInFirstWeek()</code> contiguous days in that month,
104  * ending on the day before <code>getFirstDayOfWeek()</code>.  Unlike
105  * week 1 of a year, week 1 of a month may be shorter than 7 days, need
106  * not start on <code>getFirstDayOfWeek()</code>, and will not include days of
107  * the previous month.  Days of a month before week 1 have a
108  * <code>WEEK_OF_MONTH</code> of 0.
109  *
110  * <p>For example, if <code>getFirstDayOfWeek()</code> is <code>SUNDAY</code>
111  * and <code>getMinimalDaysInFirstWeek()</code> is 4, then the first week of
112  * January 1998 is Sunday, January 4 through Saturday, January 10.  These days
113  * have a <code>WEEK_OF_MONTH</code> of 1.  Thursday, January 1 through
114  * Saturday, January 3 have a <code>WEEK_OF_MONTH</code> of 0.  If
115  * <code>getMinimalDaysInFirstWeek()</code> is changed to 3, then January 1
116  * through January 3 have a <code>WEEK_OF_MONTH</code> of 1.
117  *
118  * <p>
119  * <strong>Example:</strong>
120  * <blockquote>
121  * <pre>
122  * // get the supported ids for GMT-08:00 (Pacific Standard Time)
123  * String[] ids = TimeZone.getAvailableIDs(-8 * 60 * 60 * 1000);
124  * // if no ids were returned, something is wrong. get out.
125  * if (ids.length == 0)
126  *     System.exit(0);
127  *
128  *  // begin output
129  * System.out.println("Current Time");
130  *
131  * // create a Pacific Standard Time time zone
132  * SimpleTimeZone pdt = new SimpleTimeZone(-8 * 60 * 60 * 1000, ids[0]);
133  *
134  * // set up rules for daylight savings time
135  * pdt.setStartRule(Calendar.APRIL, 1, Calendar.SUNDAY, 2 * 60 * 60 * 1000);
136  * pdt.setEndRule(Calendar.OCTOBER, -1, Calendar.SUNDAY, 2 * 60 * 60 * 1000);
137  *
138  * // create a GregorianCalendar with the Pacific Daylight time zone
139  * // and the current date and time
140  * Calendar calendar = new GregorianCalendar(pdt);
141  * Date trialTime = new Date();
142  * calendar.setTime(trialTime);
143  *
144  * // print out a bunch of interesting things
145  * System.out.println("ERA: " + calendar.get(Calendar.ERA));
146  * System.out.println("YEAR: " + calendar.get(Calendar.YEAR));
147  * System.out.println("MONTH: " + calendar.get(Calendar.MONTH));
148  * System.out.println("WEEK_OF_YEAR: " + calendar.get(Calendar.WEEK_OF_YEAR));
149  * System.out.println("WEEK_OF_MONTH: " + calendar.get(Calendar.WEEK_OF_MONTH));
150  * System.out.println("DATE: " + calendar.get(Calendar.DATE));
151  * System.out.println("DAY_OF_MONTH: " + calendar.get(Calendar.DAY_OF_MONTH));
152  * System.out.println("DAY_OF_YEAR: " + calendar.get(Calendar.DAY_OF_YEAR));
153  * System.out.println("DAY_OF_WEEK: " + calendar.get(Calendar.DAY_OF_WEEK));
154  * System.out.println("DAY_OF_WEEK_IN_MONTH: "
155  *                    + calendar.get(Calendar.DAY_OF_WEEK_IN_MONTH));
156  * System.out.println("AM_PM: " + calendar.get(Calendar.AM_PM));
157  * System.out.println("HOUR: " + calendar.get(Calendar.HOUR));
158  * System.out.println("HOUR_OF_DAY: " + calendar.get(Calendar.HOUR_OF_DAY));
159  * System.out.println("MINUTE: " + calendar.get(Calendar.MINUTE));
160  * System.out.println("SECOND: " + calendar.get(Calendar.SECOND));
161  * System.out.println("MILLISECOND: " + calendar.get(Calendar.MILLISECOND));
162  * System.out.println("ZONE_OFFSET: "
163  *                    + (calendar.get(Calendar.ZONE_OFFSET)/(60*60*1000)));
164  * System.out.println("DST_OFFSET: "
165  *                    + (calendar.get(Calendar.DST_OFFSET)/(60*60*1000)));
166  *
167  * System.out.println("Current Time, with hour reset to 3");
168  * calendar.clear(Calendar.HOUR_OF_DAY); // so doesn't override
169  * calendar.set(Calendar.HOUR, 3);
170  * System.out.println("ERA: " + calendar.get(Calendar.ERA));
171  * System.out.println("YEAR: " + calendar.get(Calendar.YEAR));
172  * System.out.println("MONTH: " + calendar.get(Calendar.MONTH));
173  * System.out.println("WEEK_OF_YEAR: " + calendar.get(Calendar.WEEK_OF_YEAR));
174  * System.out.println("WEEK_OF_MONTH: " + calendar.get(Calendar.WEEK_OF_MONTH));
175  * System.out.println("DATE: " + calendar.get(Calendar.DATE));
176  * System.out.println("DAY_OF_MONTH: " + calendar.get(Calendar.DAY_OF_MONTH));
177  * System.out.println("DAY_OF_YEAR: " + calendar.get(Calendar.DAY_OF_YEAR));
178  * System.out.println("DAY_OF_WEEK: " + calendar.get(Calendar.DAY_OF_WEEK));
179  * System.out.println("DAY_OF_WEEK_IN_MONTH: "
180  *                    + calendar.get(Calendar.DAY_OF_WEEK_IN_MONTH));
181  * System.out.println("AM_PM: " + calendar.get(Calendar.AM_PM));
182  * System.out.println("HOUR: " + calendar.get(Calendar.HOUR));
183  * System.out.println("HOUR_OF_DAY: " + calendar.get(Calendar.HOUR_OF_DAY));
184  * System.out.println("MINUTE: " + calendar.get(Calendar.MINUTE));
185  * System.out.println("SECOND: " + calendar.get(Calendar.SECOND));
186  * System.out.println("MILLISECOND: " + calendar.get(Calendar.MILLISECOND));
187  * System.out.println("ZONE_OFFSET: "
188  *        + (calendar.get(Calendar.ZONE_OFFSET)/(60*60*1000))); // in hours
189  * System.out.println("DST_OFFSET: "
190  *        + (calendar.get(Calendar.DST_OFFSET)/(60*60*1000))); // in hours
191  * </pre>
192  * </blockquote>
